# Profile: internal-research-memo

A long-form internal-only research note: a working paper, a deep-dive on a
methodological question, an extended exploration of a phenomenon that may
or may not survive scrutiny, or a synthesis of several insights into a
coherent argument. Lower stakes than the country-diagnostic-memo (which
goes to a counterpart institution) but with more room to think out loud
and make speculative-but-flagged moves.

This is the profile to reach for when you need to write down a body of
analysis that doesn't have a single external audience yet. It is also the
profile that becomes the upstream memo behind country-diagnostic and
ministerial-briefing deliverables.

## Length target

**5,000–12,000 words.** Internal research memos are deliberately uncapped
on the upper end relative to the diagnostic memo because their purpose is
to *work through* a question, not to *present* a conclusion. Below 5,000
words the memo is probably an `insight` or a `wiki/synthesis` page,
not a memo. Above 12,000 words, split into a memo plus appendices, or
into two memos — readability falls off a cliff past that.

## Register

- **Formality:** medium. More relaxed than the country-diagnostic memo;
  the author's voice is welcome and the reasoning chain is visible. First-
  person singular is acceptable (`I find that...`), as is first-person
  plural (`we find that...`) when there are multiple authors. Speculative
  passages are explicitly flagged (`Speculative — to be tested:`).
- **Jargon:** technical jargon is fine without gloss; the audience is
  internal Lab researchers fluent in growth/development empirics.
- **Voice:** thinking-out-loud is allowed and useful. The memo can show
  the seams of the analysis (`This is the question I started with; the
  data led me to a different question, which is...`) so long as the final
  argument is clear.
- **Hedging:** liberal where warranted. Internal memos are where
  uncertain-but-promising results live before they're hardened into a
  diagnostic memo.

## Audience

Primary: other Lab researchers working on the same engagement, or peer
researchers in the Lab's broader network with a stake in the question.

Secondary: the future version of the author. Internal memos are how
analysis becomes durable; what the author wrote in May is what they will
re-read in November when the engagement enters its closing phase.

Tertiary: a hypothetical academic referee, six months later, when a
publication-quality version of part of this memo is being prepared. The
internal memo is the mulch a paper grows from.

What they need to walk away with:
- A clear statement of the question, the approach, and the findings.
- The reasoning chain — including dead ends and abandoned alternatives.
- A judgment on which findings are ready for external use and which are
  still speculative.
- Pointers to the underlying data, code, and decision records.

## Success criteria

An internal research memo lands when:

1. **The argument is traceable.** A reader can follow the analytic move
   from question → data → method → finding → implication without losing
   the thread.
2. **Speculative content is labeled as speculative.** Speculation is
   welcome; speculation passed off as established finding is the failure
   mode.
3. **The memo is internally honest.** Dead ends are reported; alternative
   interpretations are weighed; the author's prior is named when it
   shaped a methodology choice.
4. **Findings can be lifted.** A diagnostic memo or briefing built from
   this memo can cite paragraphs verbatim, because they were written to
   stand alone.
5. **Open questions are concrete.** Future-work bullets name the dataset,
   the variation, or the comparison that would resolve the question.
6. **Long-running memos accrete dated addenda; they are not rewritten.**
   When a memo's findings need updating after the original draft has
   circulated, append a `## Update: YYYY-MM-DD` section at the bottom
   (one per revision, dated, in chronological order) rather than
   editing the body of the memo in place. Rewrites discard the
   inquiry's history; addenda preserve it. The memo's original
   reasoning chain is part of what makes it useful six months later
   — the addendum says what changed and why, without erasing the
   prior version.

A memo flops when it reads as a stream-of-consciousness analysis log
(no argumentative spine), when speculative claims are presented as found
facts, when the literature it engages with is cited but not actually used,
or when the open-questions section is aspirational rather than operational.

## Recommended `/deliverable-review` lens weights

Internal memos should be balanced across the seven lenses — none dominates,
because the memo is meant to expose its own reasoning. The peer-Lab-
plausibility lens is slightly elevated (this memo will be read by peers,
and may seed external deliverables) but no other lens is downweighted to
near-zero. Robustness is medium rather than high because internal memos are
explicitly allowed to publish "this is preliminary, here is what would
falsify it" — that's a feature, not a bug.

| Lens                           | Weight | Why                                                                                  |
|--------------------------------|--------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Data validity                  | Medium-High | Sources, deflators, sample frames matter; can be slightly more provisional than in the diagnostic memo. |
| Identification / reasoning     | Medium-High | The reasoning chain is the main artifact; it must be coherent even when conclusions are tentative. |
| Robustness                     | Medium | Robustness is desirable but not required; preliminary findings can ship with explicit "this needs more checks" labels. |
| Framing                        | Medium | The memo can be exploratory in framing; it doesn't need a sharp headline. |
| Audience-fit                   | Medium-High | Internal-Lab register is the target; check that jargon and length match an internal-research audience, not a ministerial one. |
| Political-economy realism      | Medium | Some internal memos touch politically-charged questions; those need the lens. Pure methodology memos do not. |
| Peer-Lab plausibility          | Medium-High | Peers are the primary audience; the memo must be one a Lab senior researcher would defend. |

When invoking `/deliverable-review` on an internal memo, run all seven
lenses at roughly comparable weight. The memo's purpose is to expose
reasoning to scrutiny, not to land a polished conclusion — so a balanced
review is appropriate.

## Common failure modes for this profile

1. **No argumentative spine.** The memo is a series of analyses without a
   throughline; a reader can't say in one sentence what the memo is *for*.
   Fix: write the executive-summary paragraph first, even if it's
   provisional, and prune sections that don't serve it.
2. **Speculation laundered as finding.** A passage that started as "this
   is suggestive but not confirmed" loses its hedge across drafts and ends
   up read as established. Fix: explicit "Speculative — to be tested"
   markers at the top of every speculative subsection; revisit during
   review and either upgrade (with evidence) or downgrade (with explicit
   acknowledgment).
3. **Open-questions section is aspirational.** The memo ends with "future
   work could explore X, Y, Z" without naming the datasets, variations,
   or comparisons that would actually answer them. Fix: every future-work
   bullet names a concrete next step that a researcher could start on
   tomorrow morning.
