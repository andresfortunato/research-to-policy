# Profile: ministerial-briefing

A short, decision-oriented note for a minister, vice-minister, principal
secretary, or equivalent senior political official. The briefing must be
readable in the back of a car between meetings and must be defensible if a
sharper-than-expected counterpart picks at it. The political-economy stakes
are higher than the technical stakes — but a numerically wrong briefing is
a career risk for the recipient, so technical care is still required.

## Length target

**500–1,200 words.** Two pages, three at the absolute outer limit. If it
exceeds two pages, the minister will not finish reading it; if it falls
below 500 words, it likely lacks the supporting evidence to be defensible.

The briefing has a hard structure (see template): one-sentence headline,
~3 bullet findings, ~3 bullet implications/decisions-required, ~3 bullet
risks/counterarguments, "more in" pointers. Do not improvise the structure.

## Register

- **Formality:** institutional. Use the minister's title in the salutation
  if customary; otherwise no salutation. Sentences are short and complete;
  no slang, no academic hedging language ("we may tentatively conclude that
  perhaps"), no jargon the minister wouldn't use in a press conference.
- **Jargon:** strip all technical jargon. `Productivity` is fine;
  `total factor productivity` requires a one-line gloss; `Balassa-Samuelson`
  doesn't appear at all (it goes in the diagnostic memo this briefing
  is built from).
- **Voice:** institutional third-person ("The Lab assesses that...",
  "The evidence indicates..."). Avoid first-person singular. Avoid
  imperative voice toward the minister ("You should..."); use "The
  available options are..." or "A decision is required on..."
- **Numbers:** rounded. `40%`, not `39.7%`. `roughly USD 1.2 bn`, not
  `USD 1.183bn`. Precision in a briefing signals false certainty.

## Audience

Primary: the minister (or equivalent senior political official) who will
read this in 4–6 minutes between other obligations and may quote one number
from it in a press appearance the same day.

Secondary: the minister's chief of staff, principal secretary, or technical
advisor — the person who actually screens the briefing before it lands and
who will be asked, "Is this right?" The briefing must hold up to that
screening.

What they need to walk away with:
- One defensible headline they can repeat in their next conversation.
- A clear sense of what decision (if any) is being asked of them, by when.
- Two or three risks they should be ready for if pushed.
- Confidence that the underlying analysis is solid, even though they aren't
  reading it.

## Success criteria

A ministerial briefing lands when:

1. **It can be read in under five minutes** and the minister leaves with a
   one-sentence understanding they can repeat verbatim.
2. **The headline survives political contact.** If a hostile counterpart in
   the cabinet picks at the framing, the briefing's framing is still
   defensible — it is *true*, not just *useful*.
3. **The decision (or non-decision) being requested is explicit.** The
   minister knows whether they're being asked to approve, to await
   further analysis, to authorize further work, or simply to be informed.
4. **Risks and counterarguments are pre-empted.** The minister is not
   surprised in the meeting after this briefing.
5. **It has a "more in" pointer** to the diagnostic memo or research note
   it is built from, so the chief of staff can verify if needed.

A briefing flops when it reads like a memo trimmed in half (the structure
has to be redesigned, not compressed), when the headline is hedged into
mush, when the minister's first reaction is "so what?", or when a
counterpart picks at it and the supporting evidence isn't on hand.

## Recommended `/deliverable-review` lens weights

Briefings live or die on whether the framing survives political contact —
and on whether the headline is true. **Political-economy realism is the
dominant lens**, with peer-Lab plausibility a supporting check (the
underlying numbers must be defensible, but the briefing's job is not to
showcase technical sophistication).

| Lens                           | Weight | Why                                                                                  |
|--------------------------------|--------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Data validity                  | Medium | The numbers in the briefing must be right, but the briefing reports a small handful, drawn from a fully-vetted upstream memo. |
| Identification / reasoning     | Low    | The reasoning chain belongs in the diagnostic memo. The briefing reports conclusions. |
| Robustness                     | Low    | Robustness is the upstream memo's job. The briefing should not even mention "robustness checks" — it kills credibility with non-technical readers. |
| Framing                        | High   | The headline framing is the briefing's load-bearing element; weak framing = wasted ink. |
| Audience-fit                   | High   | Length, register, jargon density, decision-orientation — all must match the minister exactly. |
| **Political-economy realism**  | **High (dominant)** | Will the framing survive political contact? Are the risks pre-empted? Is the ask actionable? |
| Peer-Lab plausibility          | Medium | A peer Lab researcher should agree the briefing is *not misleading*, even if they would frame it differently for a different audience. |

When invoking `/deliverable-review` on a briefing draft, the political-
economy-realism and audience-fit and framing lenses are the load-bearing
checks; data-validity and peer-Lab-plausibility are sanity passes; the
identification and robustness lenses are quick confirmations that the
upstream memo did the work.

## Common failure modes for this profile

1. **Compressed-memo syndrome.** The author writes a 5,000-word memo, then
   trims to 1,000 — leaving the structure of a memo with the depth of a
   tweet. Fix: redesign for the briefing's hard structure (headline / 3
   findings / 3 asks / 3 risks); do not compress.
2. **Headline that flatters the minister but isn't quite true.** The
   author shades the framing toward what the minister wants to hear. Fix:
   show the draft to a peer who has not been in the room with the
   minister; if the framing fails their plausibility check, redo it.
3. **Missing "what is being asked" section.** The briefing informs but does
   not orient: the minister reads it and asks "what do you want me to do?"
   Fix: every briefing has an explicit "decision required" or "for
   information only" line.
