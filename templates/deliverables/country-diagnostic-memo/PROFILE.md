# Profile: country-diagnostic-memo

A standalone diagnostic of a country's growth, structural-transformation, or
sectoral-performance situation, written for an internal-or-peer technical
audience. The flagship analytic deliverable: dense, evidence-bearing, and
defensible under expert scrutiny.

## Length target

**4,000–7,000 words** (~10–18 pages of body text), plus an executive summary
of ≤500 words at the top. Charts and tables are inline; appendices for
methodology details, robustness, and data provenance can run another 5–10
pages but are not counted in the body target.

If the memo is shorter than 4,000 words, ask whether it should be a
`/internal-research-memo` instead. If it exceeds 7,000, split or push detail
to appendices — beyond that length the diagnostic stops being readable in one
sitting.

## Register

- **Formality:** high. Full sentences, no bullet-list-as-argument shortcuts in
  the body. Bulleted summaries are fine in the executive summary and at section
  heads.
- **Jargon:** technical jargon is welcome where it is precise (`structural
  transformation`, `Balassa-Samuelson`, `ICOR`, `revealed comparative
  advantage`); avoid jargon that signals tribe rather than precision.
- **Voice:** third-person, evidence-led. The author's voice surfaces in framing
  and in calibrated judgment ("the data are consistent with X, though Y is not
  ruled out"); never in advocacy.
- **Hedging:** explicit and quantified. "Roughly 40% of the gap" beats "much
  of the gap"; "we cannot distinguish A from B with these data" beats silence.

## Audience

Primary: a technical peer at the Lab, a counterpart researcher at a partner
institution (IMF, World Bank, central bank research department), or a
diagnostic-savvy government economist.

Secondary: the engagement's senior advisor or principal who will translate the
memo into briefing material for political principals.

What they need to walk away with:
- A defensible characterization of the country's growth/structural situation.
- Two or three diagnostic findings sharp enough to anchor a research agenda.
- Confidence that the analysis would survive a referee report.
- A clear sense of what is *not* known and where the evidence is thin.

## Success criteria

A diagnostic memo lands when:

1. **Every claim has a chart, table, or citation behind it.** A reader who
   wants to verify any sentence can do so in <60 seconds.
2. **The headline diagnosis is non-obvious.** "Country X is poor and growing
   slowly" is not a diagnosis; "X's growth slowdown since 2015 is 80%
   accounted for by labor reallocation *out* of manufacturing back into
   low-productivity services" is.
3. **Counterfactuals are explicit.** The memo says what comparator countries
   look like and why this country is or isn't on a sustainer/diverger
   trajectory.
4. **Robustness is shown, not asserted.** At least one alternative
   specification, deflator, sample restriction, or data vintage is reported
   alongside the headline.
5. **A peer reading it would push back on the *framing*, not the *numbers*.**
   If reviewers attack the data work, the memo failed. If they argue with the
   interpretation, it succeeded.

A diagnostic memo flops when it reads like a country-statistics survey, when
its findings could have been written without looking at the data, when
robustness checks are deferred to "future work", or when the headline finding
is something a generic comparative table would have surfaced.

## Recommended `/deliverable-review` lens weights

Country diagnostics live or die on technical credibility with peer
researchers. **Peer-Lab plausibility is the dominant lens**;
political-economy realism is necessary but secondary (if a finding is
politically inconvenient, that's the country's problem, not the memo's).

| Lens                           | Weight | Why                                                                                       |
|--------------------------------|--------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Data validity                  | High   | Source vintages, deflators, sample frame, missingness — diagnostic credibility starts here. |
| Identification / reasoning     | High   | The diagnostic logic (what a number means, what it doesn't) is the core analytic content. |
| Robustness                     | High   | At least one alternative spec must be shown; reviewers will hammer the headline.          |
| Framing                        | Medium | Important for the executive summary; the body is structured by the analysis, not framing. |
| Audience-fit                   | Medium | Technical register is mostly automatic for this profile; check jargon density and length. |
| Political-economy realism      | Low    | Diagnoses can and should be uncomfortable for the host government when the data warrant.  |
| **Peer-Lab plausibility**      | **High (dominant)** | The memo must survive a hypothetical referee report from a Lab senior researcher. |

When invoking `/deliverable-review` on a draft, prioritize the four High
lenses; the Medium and Low lenses are quick passes.

## Common failure modes for this profile

1. **Stylized-fact aggregation in place of diagnosis.** The memo restates
   what's in the country's IMF Article IV — readable, accurate, and
   diagnostically empty. Fix: ask "what does this say that the Article IV
   does not?"
2. **Single-spec headline with no robustness.** A regression that produces
   the marquee number is reported once; the appendix says "results are
   robust to alternative specifications" without showing them. Fix: report
   at least one alternative deflator/sample/vintage in the body, not the
   appendix.
3. **Apologetic hedging that swallows the finding.** Every sentence is
   qualified to the point that the memo says nothing. Fix: pick the two or
   three load-bearing hedges and quantify them; let the rest stand.
